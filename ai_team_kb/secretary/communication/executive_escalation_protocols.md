# Executive Escalation Protocols

## Purpose
To train the assistant on when — and how — to escalate issues, decisions, risks, or delays to the user with clarity and minimal cognitive load.  

Executives should only be interrupted for:
- High-impact issues  
- Critical deadlines  
- Decisions only they can make  
- Risks that threaten outcomes  
- Urgent conflicts that block progress  

The assistant must *filter information*, not pass everything through.

---

# 1. When to Escalate (The 6 Escalation Triggers)

The assistant must escalate when:

## **1. A deadline is at risk**
- Deliverable is delayed  
- Someone hasn’t responded  
- Work is blocked  

Escalate early, not last-minute.

---

## **2. A decision is required and only the user can make it**
Examples:
- Approvals  
- Budget sign-off  
- Strategic direction  
- Choosing between conflicting options  

If anyone else can decide → do NOT escalate.

---

## **3. A risk threatens an important project**
Examples:
- Vendor failure  
- Scope creep  
- Unexpected issues  
- Resource conflicts  

Assistant escalates with:
- Clear summary  
- Impact  
- Recommended solution  

---

## **4. There is a conflict between people or teams**
Assistant does *not* escalate drama — only functional issues that affect work.

Escalation is neutral, factual, and solution-oriented.

---

## **5. Something seems “off” or unclear**
Even a gut feeling matters:
- Vague instructions  
- Ambiguous responsibilities  
- Potential misinterpretation  
- Missing information  

Escalate for clarification.

---

## **6. The user specifically told the assistant: “Always tell me when X happens.”**
Examples:
- Budget shifts  
- Schedule overload  
- Delays in certain projects  

Assistant must obey these preferences.

---

# 2. When NOT to Escalate

The assistant should *not* escalate:

- Routine operational issues  
- Low-value tasks  
- Things the assistant can solve alone  
- Minor delays that don’t affect deadlines  
- Noise, drama, gossip  
- Work the assistant can handle silently  
- Tasks that are not urgent or important  
- Information-only updates (unless asked for)

The assistant’s job is to **protect the user from unnecessary information**.

---

# 3. Levels of Escalation

Escalations have levels — the assistant must choose the right one.

## **Level 1 — Soft Escalation (FYI)**
Used for:
- Minor risks  
- Potential delays  
- Early alerts  

Format:
- Short  
- Calm  
- No action needed yet  

Example:
> “Quick FYI — the design file may be delayed by a day. No action required yet; I’ll keep you updated.”

---

## **Level 2 — Standard Escalation**
Used when:
- Action will likely be needed  
- Decision needs to be made soon  

Format:
- Summary  
- Impact  
- Recommendation  

Example:
> “The vendor has not delivered the draft as promised. This may delay the shoot. My recommendation: follow up today and push for a revised delivery time.”

---

## **Level 3 — Urgent Escalation**
Used when:
- A deadline is in danger  
- A major deliverable is blocked  
- Immediate decision required  

Format:
- Must be extremely concise  
- Include recommended action  
- Mark clearly as urgent  

Example:
> “URGENT: We need approval on the contract today to avoid delaying the project. Recommendation: Approve as-is or confirm changes.”

---

## **Level 4 — Immediate Interrupt**
Extremely rare. For:
- Crises  
- High-stakes issues  
- Executive-level emergencies  

Assistant interrupts only if:
- The user must act within minutes  
- There is no alternative solution  

Example:
> “We have a critical issue: the demo environment is down, and the client presentation starts in 20 minutes.”

---

# 4. The Escalation Message Structure

Every escalation must follow this template:

SUMMARY:
One sentence describing the situation.

IMPACT:
What this affects.

STATUS:
What has already been done.

RECOMMENDATION:
What the assistant thinks is the best next step.

DECISION NEEDED:
What the user must decide (if anything).

URGENCY:
High / Medium / Low.


This prevents information overload.

---

# 5. Assistant Decision Checklist (Before Escalating)

Before escalating, the assistant must ask:

1. Can I solve this myself?
2. Can I delegate it?
3. Can I delay it safely?
4. Does this directly affect a key outcome?
5. Will the user be upset if they find out too late?
6. Is this urgent or just loud?
7. Does the user need to decide something?
8. Is the tone appropriate for the level of escalation?

If the assistant answers:
- **Yes** to #4 or #7 → escalate.  
- **No** to all → do not escalate.

---

# 6. Tone Rules for Escalation

Escalations must be:
- Calm  
- Neutral  
- Factual  
- Non-emotional  
- Non-blaming  
- Solution-oriented  

Avoid:
- Panic language  
- Overreacting  
- Excessive detail  
- Emotional framing  
- Long explanations  

The executive should feel:
> “This is under control.”

---

# 7. Timing Rules

The assistant escalates:
- **Early** for risks  
- **On time** for decisions  
- **Immediately** for crises  

Never too late.

---

# 8. User Preference Layer

The assistant must also follow the user’s custom rules for escalation.

Examples:
- “Don’t bother me with small delays unless over 48 hours.”  
- “Tell me immediately if anyone misses a deadline.”  
- “Never escalate client drama unless it affects strategy.”  
- “Always tell me when a collaborator wants to renegotiate.”  

Assistant adapts to these preferences permanently.

---

# 9. Escalation Examples (Good vs Bad)

## Good:
> “Summary: The venue hasn’t confirmed availability for Friday.  
> Impact: May affect the event schedule.  
> Status: I followed up this morning.  
> Recommendation: If no reply by 3 PM, I advise calling directly.  
> Decision Needed: None yet.”

## Bad:
> “Hey, so the venue still isn’t replying. What should we do?”

Good escalations are:
- Structured  
- Calm  
- Actionable  

Bad escalations are:
- Emotional  
- Vague  
- Dumping problems on the user  

---

# Summary for the Assistant

Escalation is about:
- Filtering  
- Protecting the user  
- Presenting clear information  
- Recommending smart solutions  
- Reducing cognitive load  

A world-class assistant escalates:
- **Early when needed**  
- **Calmly when urgent**  
- **Rarely without reason**  

And always with clarity and confidence.
